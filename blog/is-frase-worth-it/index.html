The Real Test: Replacing Manual Research With Automated Briefs

Content brief creation is one of the most time-consuming, least enjoyable parts of SEO writing — pulling competitor structure, headings, and questions manually before a single word gets written. Frase's pitch is automating that step. The real test is whether the automated brief is actually good enough to skip the manual version.

Method: Used Frase's Solo plan for a month producing real articles, building an AI brief for each one from competitor analysis, then comparing the time spent against a normal manual research process, and separately ran the same target keywords through Surfer SEO for a side-by-side comparison.

Result: Automated briefs correctly identified the core topics, common headings, and a solid starting question list for nearly every article — cutting research time substantially versus doing it by hand. They weren't perfect: a few briefs needed manual additions for less common but important subtopics competitors had covered. Against Surfer, Frase's brief-building was faster and more automated; Surfer's live in-editor scoring while writing was more precise for real-time optimization.

Frase and Surfer solve different parts of the same problem — brief automation before writing versus live scoring during writing. Several testers ended up wanting both, not one instead of the other.

Frase vs Surfer SEO: Brief Automation vs Live Scoring

Frase's strength is compressing the research and brief-building stage — it automates work that otherwise takes real manual time before writing even starts.

Surfer's strength is live, in-editor NLP scoring while writing, giving real-time feedback as content gets drafted rather than a one-time brief upfront.

Teams with the budget for both get faster research and tighter live optimization; teams choosing one should pick based on which stage of the workflow actually causes the most friction today.

Final verdict: Frase is worth it for content teams publishing regularly enough to feel research time as a real cost. Use the $1 trial on a real article you're about to write, not a throwaway test topic.
